Physicians spend four and a half hours every day on documentation. More time than they spend with patients.

This is Voice to FHIR — a proof of concept that uses Google's Med-Gemma to transform voice dictation into structured E-H-R data automatically.

The problem: clinicians speak naturally, but E-H-Rs require structured data with medical codes. Manual entry is slow and burns out physicians.

The solution: A-I-powered extraction that listens to clinical dictation and automatically populates the E-H-R.

Here's a live demo. A 48-year-old male with chest pain on aspirin. I'll send the clinical note to the extraction A-P-I.

In under three seconds, Med-Gemma extracts the complete picture: diagnosis of anjainuh with I-C-D-10 code, medications with R-X-Norm codes, vital signs, and orders for stress testing and statin therapy.

We benchmarked against rule-based extraction on 199 clinical entities. Med-Gemma achieves 69 percent F-1 — a 13 point improvement over baseline. Critically, Med-Gemma catches allergies and family history that rules completely miss — zero percent baseline on both.

This is a proof of concept, and 69 percent is honest. But we've mapped the path to production: prompt engineering for orders, A-S-R preprocessing, and scaling to Med-Gemma 27-B. Our analysis projects 78 to 83 percent F-1 — where reviewing extractions becomes faster than entering data from scratch.

The system exports to F-H-I-R, C-D-A, or H-L-7 for any E-H-R system. Deploys to the cloud at three cents per extraction, or on-premises for two thousand dollars — a fraction of annual scribe costs.

Voice to FHIR. Open source. A working proof of concept with a clear path to production. Because physicians should spend their time with patients, not paperwork.
